Denise Brooks, PhD

I am a bioinfomatician, data analyst and research programmer working at the forefront of cancer research.

About Me

Genomic sequencing is my scientific adventure. My first sequence readout came from microbes collected by a submarine from a deep sea hydrothermal vent. I was hooked. Since then I've explored the lives of microbes living in boiling acid and in forest soils, used RNA and methylation like a detective to deduce the sex lives of ants, and am now using all my knowledge and skills to help defeat cancer.

Work

Canada's Michael Smith Genome Sciences Centre, Vancouver, BC Canada

Computational Biologist • Apr 2013 - present

I research, design, and implement custom statistical tools for production and research analyses, including development of visualization tools to translate analytics into information that is accessible to production staff and researchers: modeling, multivariate regression, differential expression, feature correlation, and clustering for quality assessments and tumour subtype discovery. Design and implement production pipeline workflows for high-throughput sequence data in a cluster-computing environment, including writing documentation and performing validations. Write content and perform analyses that make important contributions to scientific papers and am included as a contributing author for those analyses.

TBRC Center of Molecular Biosciences, University of the Ryukyus, Okinawa Japan

Invited Foreign Researcher, JSPS fellowship• Oct 2012 - Apr 2013

Wrote successful grant to fund a collaborative high-throughput sequencing project to characterize the long-term effects of assisted tree species migration on soil microbial communities. Developed a high-throughput sequencing strategy to characterize soil bacterial, fungal, archaeal, and protist communities at high coverage using a single sequencing run in collaboration with Prof Jenke-Kodama, Microbiology and Biochemistry of Secondary Metabolites Unit, OIST.

Ecology and Evolution Unit, Okinawa Institute of Science and Technology, Okinawa Japan

Researcher•Oct 2010 to Oct 2012

De novo genome of the little fire ant: Researched, installed, and tested a wide range of bioinformatics software on the cluster computing resource under Suse and Red Hat Scientific Linux. Generated initial de novo assembly and annotation of the little fire ant transcriptome. Wrote custom scripts to analyze differential gene expression in the context of gene methylation. Tool suites and methods included: Maker, Abyss, TransAbyss, Biopython, Samtools, Bioperl, BioConductor, local and custom BLAST and Blast2Go database construction, STACKS RAD-seq (including web enabled install), bowtie, cufflinks, and tophat. The evolution of eusociality (using World of Warcraft as a hypothesis testing engine): Wrote program in Java that spawned processes to automatically log avatars in and out of realms, canvas avatars sharing a gaming instance, and crawl the on-line armory to collect wealth and guild membership statistics for each player. Network analysis indicated that increases in guild wealth were correlated with higher connectedness between players. Coding languages used: Java, Lua, Python and R.

Education

University of British Columbia, Canada

PhD Forestry, Forest Science • Nov 2010

Innovated, designed, and promoted novel techniques for studying forest soil microbial community structure and function in the context of forest regeneration. Designed software for the analysis of an in situ phosphatase assay that demonstrated qualitative differences in soil enzyme activity and wrote programs to facilitate the processing and analysis of TRFLP, DGGE and PLFA data files. Designed, organized, and supervised four field-based research programs utilizing a chronosequence of forest stands located throughout south central British Columbia. Wrote six successful funding applications including a proposal that funded two years of PhD research and salaries for two science coop students. Mentored graduate students, supervised field and laboratory assistants including management of two science co-op student researchers in the production of a scientific paper and an award winning research poster.
